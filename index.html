01 The Setup

The right race, the wrong engine.

By early 2025, OOFOS was sitting in an enviable category and a difficult position at the same time. The recovery footwear market was growing fast. Search interest was up roughly 150% over five years, and the forecasts pointed to continued expansion through 2026. OOFOS had genuine product-market fit, a loyal base, and a brand customers actively recommended to friends. The product wasn't the problem.

The growth engine was.

Q1 2025 closed with revenue down 33% YoY. New customer acquisition was down 55%. Returning customer revenue was carrying the topline while the new-customer funnel quietly drained. BFCM 2024 had already come in 19% below the prior year, which was the early warning that the issue wasn't seasonal noise. Something structural was breaking.

When Force of Nature ran its initial Opportunity Analysis, the diagnosis was uncomfortable in its specificity. Roughly 70% of Google Ads spend was going to brand terms, capturing demand that already existed rather than generating new demand. Over $800K of Meta spend was reaching existing customers, with frequency caps blown past 46 impressions per user. About 28% of TikTok budget was running against retargeting, on a platform whose entire mechanic is discovery. Linear TV was eating up to 41% of media mix in peak months, and it correlated negatively with new cohort acquisition.

Channel scorecards across the board sat at 5/10 or 6/10. The accounts weren't broken. They were over-optimized for the wrong thing. Almost every dollar was being spent to harvest existing intent, and almost no dollars were being spent to generate it.

OOFOS is in the right race, but the engine is misfiring.

02 The Diagnosis

Two questions, three phases.

Force of Nature's Opportunity Analysis didn't open with a list of tactics. It opened with two questions.

Are we doing it right? How much more demand can we capture?

The first question forced an honest channel-by-channel audit. The second forced the team to stop benchmarking against last quarter and start benchmarking against the addressable market.

Out of those two questions came a three-phase plan. Phase 1 was about getting the engine running right: fixing the media inefficiencies, deploying channel best practices, shifting focus from retargeting to net-new acquisition, and consolidating the fragmented account structures. Phase 2 was about capturing the full market opportunity, which meant building forecasts, optimizing seasonal allocation, and realigning the media mix away from low-impact broad reach into high-intent networks. Phase 3 was about installing a data-driven system: moving from reactive marketing to real-time decision-making, prioritizing long-term profitability over short-term ROAS, and putting incrementality at the center of measurement.

The framing matters because it set expectations correctly from day one. The mandate wasn't to make 2025 outperform 2024. It was to stop the bleeding, rebuild the acquisition engine, and put a system in place that compounds. Twelve months later, the Q1 2026 numbers told the story of all three phases executing in sequence.

i.
The Rebuild
Q2 2025 · April—June

The first ninety days were structural. Not a campaign refresh. Not a creative overhaul. An account-level reset across every paid channel.

Meta was running ten or more active campaigns simultaneously, splitting algorithmic learning across too many surfaces to ever build liquidity on any of them. Force of Nature consolidated to five campaigns, then four. Reservation-based campaigns were disabled in favor of auction. Spend allocated to existing customers, which sat above 25% of the total at engagement start, was reduced systematically to 17.7% by end of June, with further reductions on the roadmap. By June, more than half of Meta spend had shifted to dynamic product ads, which were delivering CACs of $28.27 against an account average of $30.46.

Google Ads was fragmented across dozens of low-budget campaigns, none of which had enough daily spend to escape the algorithm's learning phase. The team consolidated by funnel stage and seasonality, removed budget caps where possible, and let ROAS targets govern delivery. Branded retargeting wasn't eliminated, but it stopped being the strategy.

TikTok had been running 30%+ of budget against the 55+ age cohort and treating the platform like a remarketing channel. Spend to existing customers was cut to zero. The 55+ allocation dropped to 21% by end of Q2. Spark Ads with creator handles became the format of focus.

DV360 had been running YouTube only. Display, video, and CTV came online with audience segmentation across publisher custom segments, custom affinity, and intent, finally using the platform as a top-funnel tool rather than a YouTube delivery mechanism.

By June the structural surgery was visible in the numbers. Prospecting spend was up +220% YoY in May and 98% in June. The YoY new customer gap had closed from −54% in Q1 to −10% in Q2. Q2 revenue landed at −9.4% YoY, compared to −33% in Q1. Prospecting share of paid media had jumped from roughly 7% to 37% of the mix.

The trade-off was real, and it was named honestly. nCAC ticked up 16% YoY in May before settling at +2.88% in June. That was the cost of buying back acquisition velocity. It was the right cost to pay.

ii.
The Breakthrough
Q3 2025 · July—September

Q3 was when the rebuild stopped looking like a recovery and started looking like a system.

New customers grew +117% YoY in August and 105% in September. Revenue grew 72% in August and 38% in September. nCAC moved to −10.98% YoY in August, which was the first month efficiency moved with volume rather than against it.

The August End-of-Season Sale exceeded forecasts to the point operations had to throttle volume because the warehouse couldn't ship fast enough. That moment, more than any single number on a dashboard, signalled the engine was running.

Two strategic principles crystallized in Q3 and have governed every decision since.

The first: paid is for acquisition, owned is for retention. Once the team stopped trying to make paid media do both jobs, both jobs got easier. Prospecting share of paid budget pushed to 90—95%. Email and SMS picked up the returning-customer volume that retargeting had been awkwardly subsidizing. Each channel started doing what it was structurally good at.

The second: scale is driven upstream, not manufactured at the point of conversion. Cohort growth in Q3 wasn't the result of better landing pages or smarter retargeting windows. It was the result of more sessions, more brand impressions, more cold-audience reach. The work happens at the top of the funnel and the lift propagates down.

iii.
The Pressure Test
Q4 2025 · October—December

A rebuilt engine that performs in calm conditions is a hypothesis. Q4 was the stress test.

The 90/95 prospecting split held throughout. There was no defensive retreat to retargeting when budgets got tight. New customer growth continued uninterrupted year-over-year every month from August onward.

The BFCM execution was where the strategy proved itself most clearly. Rather than treating BFCM as a single promotional moment, the team split it into phases, with a build-up window starting November 9th designed to seed the funnel before the actual promo launch. Conversions during build-up lagged on purpose, because the leading indicators being watched were brand impressions and prospecting reach, not daily revenue.

Black Friday delivered an all-time high revenue, up 12.08% YoY. Day 1 of the promo period came in 65% above forecast at roughly $27 nCAC and 7x ROAS. November 24 finished 63.94% above forecast. Black Friday itself drove a 45.38% returning customer uplift alongside a 44.48% new customer uplift.

The October Cozy Sale, a smaller test of the same playbook, had already validated it. Four promo days closed at $37.68 nCAC and 4.71 DTC ROAS, with more than 6,000 new customers and 8,000 returning customers acquired during the window.

Owned channels handled the returning-customer volume during loyalty promos so cleanly that retargeting spend could be held nearly flat throughout, varying by less than $400 across the entire promo period. That single data point validated the "Paid = Acquisition, Owned = Retention" split more than any deck slide could.

03 The Strategic Backbone

Incrementality as the system of record.

This is the part of the engagement that prospective clients should look at most closely.

By Q4 2025, with $3.5M+ flowing across seven active channels, last-click ROAS had stopped being a useful decision-making tool. It systematically over-credits channels that intercept existing intent, like branded search, retargeting, and lower-funnel display. It systematically under-credits channels that build that intent in the first place, like CTV, TikTok, and upper-funnel programmatic.

If budget allocation gets driven by last-click reporting, the channels that build demand will lose budget to the channels that harvest it. Eventually there's no demand left to harvest. That is the slow-motion failure mode that ends with declining cohorts and rising CACs, and it's exactly what was playing out at OOFOS in early 2025.

Force of Nature's response was to install HAUS geo-holdout testing as the measurement spine, complemented by Triple Whale for multi-touch attribution. HAUS methodology is structurally simple. You expose one set of geographic markets to a channel, hold another set out, and measure the difference in business outcomes. The output is an incrementality coefficient: what revenue the business would have actually lost if that channel hadn't existed.

That changes the budget allocation conversation entirely. Instead of comparing platform-reported ROAS numbers that all use different attribution windows and self-credit conversions, every dollar's marginal return can be modeled against every other dollar's marginal return on a consistent basis. Budget allocation becomes a P&L optimization problem rather than a reporting comparison.

The Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 testing program included CTV incrementality testing on Roku and Axon, which together accounted for over $307K of Q1 2026 spend with no meaningful last-click attribution. The only way to know whether that money was working was to measure it incrementally. Meta scale incrementality testing answered a different question: at what spend level does Meta begin experiencing diminishing incremental returns? The team can now model this directly rather than infer it from CPA drift. Cross-channel halo testing examined whether TikTok spend lifts Meta conversion rates, and whether CTV pre-loads audiences that Meta then converts. The hypothesis that TikTok creates awareness, Meta captures intent, and Google closes can now be tested rather than assumed.

AppLovin's first HAUS test came in below the 1.5x iROAS target, which the team interpreted as over-scaling rather than structural failure. Pinterest's October test posted a positive 1.02 iROAS even in low-demand conditions, with a retest planned for early Q2 2026 against a 1.5x target.

The strategic point isn't that incrementality testing produced specific channel decisions. The point is that the entire budget allocation conversation now happens on different terms. That's a fundamentally different operating model from "which campaign has the best ROAS this week."

04 The Demand Engine

One foundation, five surfaces.

The other tactical foundation worth surfacing is how Force of Nature reorganized creative and content strategy around a single underlying logic.

The framework rests on four immutable questions. Who are we talking to? Why do they care? What product or solution fits them? What benefit actually matters to them?

From those four questions, the team builds Demand Themes, which are the intersection of audience, angle, and product. Audiences get scored by search demand, market size, and seasonal lift. Angles get evaluated by total search volume, growth trajectory, and seasonality. Products get prioritized by business seasonality, revenue contribution, and conversion role.

A single Demand Theme — for example, "Injured Athletes × Comfort × Slides" — then becomes the source of truth that powers five distinct execution surfaces: paid creative across Meta, TikTok, Pinterest, and DV360; landing pages built to convert paid traffic and rank organically; SEO topical clusters with supporting content hubs feeding internal links; organic social distribution that repurposes paid creative themes; and AI search visibility through entity coverage and structured signals across the open web.

This matters tactically because it solves the creative volume problem at scale. At $1.91M in Meta spend per quarter, industry benchmarks suggest 30 to 50 active creative variants are required to avoid algorithm fatigue and audience burnout. Producing that volume from scratch is impossible. Producing it from a structured Demand Theme matrix, with AI-assisted variant generation and an always-on creator network, is achievable.

Creative is no longer "ideas." It's systematic coverage of demand narratives.

05 The Payoff

Three levers, one operating system.

Three Q1 2026 levers tell the tactical story underneath the headline numbers.

The structural shift from a roughly 51/49 prospecting/retargeting split to 95/5 was the single most consequential decision of the quarter. January 2026's nCAC of $28.04 was the lowest single month in account history. If Q1 2026's volume had been acquired at Q1 2025's nCAC, total spend would have been roughly $1.5M higher for identical output.

Meta operated as the primary acquisition engine. Q1 2026 Meta spend was $1.91M, up 59% year-over-year, at a $34.42 average nCAC. Efficiency held across the ramp: January nCAC at $28, February at $37, March at $38. Volume at scale, not volume at any price.

Google moved in the opposite direction, with efficiency taking priority over scale. Q1 2026 Google spend was $342K lower than Q1 2025, while channel-attributed revenue grew 51%. ROAS moved from 4.6x to 9.1x. Bing ($72K) and YouTube ($81K) were cut entirely, with the freed budget redeployed into Meta and upper-funnel brand channels.

The cohorts entering the retention funnel in Q1 2026 were the largest in OOFOS company history. The monthly average climbed to 34,756 new customers, compared to 19,562 in Q1 2025. Every retention intervention going forward operates on a customer base nearly twice the size.

What's next.

The most credible signal that this isn't a "we won" story is that Force of Nature is already pointing at what's next.

Q1 2026 surfaced a checkout gap. Checkouts initiated grew 310% year-over-year, from 217K to 890K. The checkout-to-purchase rate compressed from 52.9% to 19.6%. The team estimates that a conservative recovery scenario, which would mean moving checkout-to-purchase back to 35%, would have added approximately $4.2M in revenue in Q1 alone, at zero additional media spend.

That's not a failure of the acquisition engine. It's a different problem entirely, sitting downstream of the work that's been done. It's also the single largest revenue opportunity for Q2 2026. Q1 was about building the acquisition engine. Q2 is about extracting value from what it produces.

The compounding logic is also already in motion. Paid customer acquisition gets more expensive at scale. Organic acquisition gets cheaper. Building organic infrastructure now, through the same Demand Theme foundation that powers paid, means lower blended nCAC at every future growth milestone.

The Takeaway

The headline outcome was 78% more new customers at 30% lower nCAC. The underlying outcome was an operating system.

That's the difference between a campaign that worked and a partnership that compounds.
